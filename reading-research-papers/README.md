# Reading research papers

## Context

Often to stay on top of modern technologies, you'd have to read scientific papers in way that:

- does not take too much time;
- it beneficial for you in the sense that you can use what is written there to accomplish your goals faster and with greater area of impact;
- makes it easy to share what you've learned with others.

## Problems

Papers are often:

- hard to read;
- have lots of mathematics in them;
- can be quite long;
- do not present a common structure;
- too theoretical in nature;
- have sections with mixed added value (some - low, some - high) - identifying which is which is not easy.

## Solution

Do iterative/incremental reads, focusing on different sections each time.

Here is a recipe:

- Category: language models, recommender systems, agentic AI, etc
- What did the authors try to accomplish? ...one sentence summarizing the main contribution...
- What were the key elements of the approach? ...concrete steps to reproduce the contribution...
- What can you use yourself? ...any notes that are worth taking/perspective shifts...
- What other references do you want to follow? ...recommended reading for more context...
- What are some limitations? ...points that should be mentioned as nothing is perfect...

Notes:

- Have a "qualifying round" for papers: first skim them and only then pick the papers on which you're going to focus.
  - Look at the figures and tables in the first read - if you cannot understand the paper by looking at the graphs and tables, skip/ignore it.
- Do not focus on the math at first reading: only if you want to make sure you really, really understand it.
- If you really like the paper, try to rederive it (math, code, experiments).

## Benefits

- A structure that you can apply to every paper.
- A way to actually learn and take effective notes.

## Todo for oneself

Try this recipe on several papers.
